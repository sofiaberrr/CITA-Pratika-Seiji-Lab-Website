Illuminating the First Billion Years

MISSION

To catalyze and sustain world-leading research on the formation and evolution of galaxies and black holes in the early Universe — through coordinated observation, theory, instrumentation, and data science — positioning Toronto as a defining international hub for high-redshift astrophysics.

DRAFT — MARCH 2026

Coordinators: S. Fujimoto (DADDAA), P. Dayal (CITA), A. Muzzin (York), J. Speagle (Statistics/DADDAA)

Overview

The University of Toronto occupies a uniquely powerful position in global efforts to understand the first billion years of cosmic history.

Few institutions worldwide combine — within a single ecosystem — a historic department of astronomy (DADDAA), a nationally funded theoretical powerhouse (CITA), an endowed institute with strengths in instrumentation and observational leadership (Dunlap Institute), and one of the world's leading AI and statistical science communities (Vector Institute, DSI). Toronto is one of the rare places where education, theory, observations, instrumentation, large-scale surveys, and data science are structurally integrated.

This moment is particularly timely. Toronto-based researchers are leading major JWST large programs that are defining the observational frontier of the high-redshift Universe. CITA has entered a new phase following its 40th anniversary, with renewed faculty growth in cosmology and galaxy formation theory. Cross-appointments with statistics and data science are embedding advanced AI/machine learning methodologies directly into astrophysical discovery. At the same time, next-generation facilities (SKA, LISA, ELTs) are poised to transform our understanding of early structure formation across electromagnetic and gravitational-wave windows.

Despite these strengths, there is currently no dedicated framework that unifies Toronto's observational, theoretical, computational, and instrumental leadership around the physics of the early Universe.

Aurora will establish a virtual, cross-disciplinary institute focused specifically on galaxy and black hole formation during cosmic dawn and reionization. By coordinating existing strengths across UofT and the Greater Toronto Area, the initiative will consolidate Toronto's position as an international hub for high-redshift astrophysics — not merely as a collection of strong groups, but as a coherent global center.

Scientific Focus

Goals & Research

QUESTION 01

How did the first galaxies form and assemble their stellar mass?

The populations JWST has uncovered at z > 10 sit in tension with existing models of star formation and feedback in low-mass, low-metallicity halos. Aurora brings together deep panchromatic survey data, cosmological simulations, and Bayesian population inference to build a coherent physical picture of early galaxy assembly.

QUESTION 02

How did supermassive black holes grow so rapidly?

Luminous AGN at z > 7 imply massive black holes were in place within a few hundred million years of the Big Bang. Aurora connects high-redshift AGN observations with theoretical models of seed formation, accretion physics, and AGN–galaxy co-evolution, using statistical methods to infer black hole demographics from heterogeneous survey data.

QUESTION 03

How did reionization proceed, and what drove it?

The timing, morphology, and drivers of reionization encode the properties of ionizing sources and the structure of the intergalactic medium. Aurora combines panchromatic observations (UV escape fractions, Lyα transmission, 21 cm signals) with simulations of inhomogeneous reionization and data science methods for jointly constraining source and IGM properties.

I
Observations

Aurora members lead major JWST programs defining the observational frontier of the high-redshift universe, including large treasury and GTO surveys, alongside programs on ground-based facilities including the ELTs and SKA.

II
Theory & Simulation

Drawing on CITA at UofT and the computational astrophysics group at York, Aurora provides capacity in cosmological theory, radiation hydrodynamics, galaxy formation modeling, and large-scale numerical simulations of reionization and early structure formation.

III
Data Science & Statistics

Researchers with joint appointments between Statistical Sciences and DADDAA embed Bayesian inference, machine learning, and survey methodology directly into astrophysical research — extracting physical insight from complex, high-dimensional datasets.

IV
AGN & Black Holes

Faculty at both institutions work on quasar outflows, AGN variability, and black hole mass measurements — connecting directly to Aurora's essential question on early supermassive black hole growth, with complementary expertise in accretion physics and spectroscopic diagnostics.

Aurora's cross-institutional nature is not incidental — it is a defining feature. Coordinators are drawn from both universities and span observations, theory, and statistics. Both institutions bring large and active groups of students and postdocs in high-z science, anchored by leading JWST survey teams. Programs & Training

Activities

MEETINGS

Weekly Group Meetings

Joint high-z group meetings drawing participants from DADDAA, Dunlap, and CITA, with rotating presenters from different pillars. Includes a galaxy journal club series coordinated across groups.

VISITORS

International Visitors Program

Funded short-term visits of 2–8 weeks for international collaborators, strengthening Aurora's global research network and providing trainees with direct exposure to leading researchers worldwide.

WORKSHOPS

Workshops & Conferences

A flagship international workshop every 2–3 years, with smaller topical meetings annually. These raise Aurora's profile and create sustained opportunities for cross-institutional collaboration.

TRAINING

Co-Mentorship Model

Every Aurora trainee — graduate student or postdoctoral fellow — works with at least two mentors drawn from different research pillars. This is not a committee structure; it is a genuine collaborative relationship in which the trainee's research lives at the intersection of the mentors' expertise. Trainees also participate in an annual internal review and a postdoc lecture series.

FELLOWSHIPS

Fellowship Extensions

Aurora offers a funded +1 year extension to holders of competitive 2-year external fellowships — UofT A&S, Schmidt AI, DSI, Research Excellence, and international awards including Rubicon, JSPS, Marie-Curie, Humboldt, SNSF, UKRI, CSC, NRF, NSTC, A*STAR, and SERB/ANRF — making Toronto directly competitive with longer offers elsewhere.
